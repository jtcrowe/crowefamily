Introduction

5th December 2023 at 3:17pm

Over the years I have pulled together various documents relating to my family history. Some were passed down from my parents and other relatives. Some I found on the internet through various genealogy sites. I entered a lot of data into my genealogy software, but came to realize that this isn't a user friendly way to present the family history and the stories that are so important to understanding our ancestors. So I decided to take a stab at creating this document.

There is a lot of family lore and mythology in the Crowe family history. My Dad loved to tell the family stories about the Crowes in Leadville, Colorado, and how they got there from Ireland. On the few occasions when I was present for the family gatherings with my uncles, even more stories were told. Sometimes those stories contradicted other stories. And when they started talking about this James Crowe and that James Crowe, things got really confusing.

They weren't always sure how those two Crowes were related. The theory was that the younger James was the nephew of the elder James, but there was no documentation. When the Irish Catholic parish baptismal records were made available, I found the younger James' baptismal record. This was quite a find, because now I had his parents' names. His father was Daniel Crowe, a person whom I had never heard my Dad or my uncles talk about and who did not emigrate to the U.S. as did his brothers and his oldest son. There is a possible link for us back to Ireland through his descendants, but I have not researched this branch of the family tree.

One of the most persistent stories was that we were directly related to a whaling ship captain, Job Davis. I learned that this is a true story, and, though we have many direct ancestors by the name of Job Davis, the whaling ship captain was the nephew of John Davis.

Another story is that the Crowes came into the U.S. through New Orleans. I have not found any documentation of this, however. James Crowe's ship landed in New York City. Patrick Crowe's ship landed in Philadelphia. James the younger left Ireland at the age of 14. I have not found a passenger list with his name on it. Another possibility is that Mary Cassidy Crowe's father, James Cassidy, or her mother, Margaret Flanagan, came through New Orleans. I have found some New Orleans passenger lists that list a James Crowe, but I'm reluctant to say this is a record of our ancestor.

The biggest discovery I have made to date is the birth record of Thomas John Johnston. Thomas was adopted by the Johnstons. I have not seen his birth certificate but it likely names the Johnstons as his parents. However, mom, Margaret Johnston Crowe, reminded the family that Thomas' original name was John Thomas and there is an affidavit testifying to that fact. We know from the marriage record of Thomas and Nellie Bartholomew that his biological parents were Bernard Thomas and Bell Rice. I always assumed that was the end of the story until I noticed a record online of the birth of John Tomas, whose parents were Bernard and Bell Tomas. Ireland is given as Bernard's place of birth. A little more research revealed that Tomas is the Gaelic spelling of Thomas. After Bell's death in Detroit, Bernard placed Thomas and his sister Margaret in an orphanage, and was never heard from again. Did he return to Ireland? Long sought answers often lead to even more questions.

My genealogy research owes a great deal to the contributions of others. I owe a great debt to my great aunt Glenn who researched the Bartholomew family. Monty Corley's researches into the Crowe family steered me in the right direction when I was at a loss to understand the Irish and American Crowes and he provided a wealth of family stories. My recent email conversations with Val Kaleda opened the Davis family history to me. My nephew, Chris Busacker, did an excellent job in a school report compiling details of the lives of his maternal grandparents, Thomas and Margaret Crowe. And of course many conversations with my uncles Bill and Dan Crowe and Thomas Johnston guided me.

I have limited the scope of this book to my parents' generation and the preceding generations in a direct line, going as far back in time as possible until reliable documentation runs out. I have made exceptions for ancestors who figure in family lore, such as Job Davis the whaling captain and the younger James Crowe and his father Daniel.

Hyperlinks are sprinkled liberally throughout the text to make it easy to navigate our families and ancestors.

Notes

James Crowe emigrated to the US from Ireland in 1875. The manifest of the steamer RMS City of Chester, written on Feb. 17 of that year, lists James Crowe, age 28, occupation laborer, The steamer departed from Liverpool and Queenstown, England. He got to Leadville, CO, in 1878.

James worked as a teamster. The Crowe family operated ore teams there until the railroad came to town. Then they operated a feed and grain store. (By some accounts it was Ady & Crowe Mercantile even then.)

He married Mary Ann Cassidy in 1878. They lived in Salt Lake City from 1909 to 1912. The CroweFamily moved to Denver in 1899. Some accounts relate that is when they started the Ady & Crowe Mercantile Company. James and Mary Ann moved to Denver in 1912. James Crowe worked at the grain and feed business and operated a ranch in the Greeley area.

(The newspaper obituary that William Crowe had in his possession gives his birth year as 1845, but his grave marker says 1849. If the ship's manifest is correct that he was 28 in 1875, then he was probably born in 1847 or 1846. However, his baptismal record has it that he was baptized on July 15, 1849.)

Notes from Margaret Crowe (Johnston): “Thomas Johnston was born John Thomas. His father placed him and his sister Margaret in a Detroit orphanage after their mother died when he has 8 years old. His father put him and his two sisters Mayme and Margaret in a Catholic Orphanage. Mayme was placed with a family from Grosse Point. Supposedly his father was going to return for them after he got back on his feet, but this did not happen. The Johnstons adopted him and Margaret when he was around 8 or 9 years old. Their home was in Kalamazoo, MI. Margaret died while a young girl. Mrs. Johnston adopted another girl, Frances. The family also lived in New Kirk, OK, and Peoria, IL.

The story goes that his classmates at school made fun of his name, so the Johnstons changed his name to Thomas John Johnston. He was raised by this family and attended school until 8th grade in Kalamazoo, MI. After 8th grade he became an apprentice as a finisher in the photo-engraving trade in Peoria. Mr. Johnston traveled much of the time selling farm machinery in the West. Mrs. Johnston adopted another girl, Anna. After working a number of years in Michigan, Thomas took a job in Cedar Rapids, IA, at the Tru-Art Shop. Then after working there he worked at the Commercial Art for Mr. Frank Dove.

Thomas enlisted as Private in the US Army on June 26, 1918 at Peoria, IL. He sailed from Camp Mills on October 5, 1918. He arrived in France on October 13. From his diary: 'Started to the front lines November 11 at 8 a.m. at 9 a.m. got word that the armistice was signed and went back to barracks. Stayed there one week.' Arrived back in US at Newport News on Mya 24, 1919. He served in the 29th division. He was discharged at Camp Pike, Arkansas, on June 3, 1919. After discharge he went back to work for Frank Dove at Commercial Art and Engraving in Cedar Rapids. Through his boss he met Nell Bartholomew.

Thomas Johnston and Nell Bartholomew were married September 27, 1922 in St. Patrick's Church, Cedar Rapids. They took the train to California, stopping for a time in Pueblo, CO, to visit Nell's sister Glenn (also known as Fern). In 1923 their first child, Margaret, was born in Mercy Hospital, Cedar Rapids. Thomas' right leg and foot were affected. It was necessary for him to learn to walk again. He bought an Essex and Nellie learned to drive it so she could get him to work. In Oregon they bought a small house and barn near Eugene where they raised white leghorn chickens, selling the eggs to the grocery stores. They children were enrolled in St. Mary's Catholic school, Eugene. in 1942 Thomas went by bus to Tulsa, Oklahoma, in hopes of finding work in his trade.”
